# Learning Approach

A dual-purpose approach to building technical understanding across [Data Management](../data_management/), [Project Management](../project_management/), and [Software Development](../software_development/). The same four stages serve as a **sequential learning path** for long-term expertise development and a **decision-making lens** for practical problem-solving. The progression draws on research in skill acquisition [1], learning science [2], and the psychology of expertise [3].

---

## Development

Follow sequentially; each stage builds on the previous.

### 1. Orientation: *Map the landscape and context*

- **What is it?** Name it, place it in context
- **What problem does it solve?** Understand why it exists, not just what it does
- **What are the main areas?** Identify core subdomains, components, or building blocks
- **How does it relate to what I know?** Anchor new knowledge to existing understanding

Skipping orientation is one of the most common mistakes. Without it, you risk solving the wrong problem, duplicating existing work, or building in a direction that doesn't fit the broader landscape.

### 2. Foundations: *Learn concepts, rules, and relationships*

- **What are the core concepts?** Learn the fundamental building blocks and vocabulary
- **How do the parts fit together?** Understand relationships, data flows, and dependencies
- **What does the syntax/interface look like?** Read and write basic examples to build familiarity
- **What are the rules and constraints?** Know what is required, optional, and forbidden

Strong foundations let you read documentation faster, debug more efficiently, and recognize patterns across different technologies. Without them, you're constantly guessing rather than reasoning. Research on effective learning confirms that retrieval practice and interleaving outperform passive re-reading [2].

### 3. Application: *Apply under real constraints and trade-offs*

- **When should I use this, and when shouldn't I?** Recognize appropriate use cases given real-world constraints
- **What are the trade-offs?** Every choice has costs: performance vs readability, speed vs safety, cost vs capability
- **What constraints shape the solution?** Requirements, compliance, stakeholder needs, budget, team capacity, existing systems
- **What does this look like in practice?** Move from isolated examples to realistic enterprise scenarios
- **What are the common pitfalls?** Learn what goes wrong and why, before it happens to you

This is where theory meets reality. The best solution on paper often fails under budget, timeline, or organizational constraints. Knowing *how* something works is not the same as knowing *when* and *why* to use it.

### 4. Judgment: *Develop reasoning through ambiguity*

- **What is the right approach for this context?** Navigate ambiguity with competing stakeholder needs and constraints
- **What am I assuming, and could I be wrong?** Identify blind spots, biases, and unvalidated assumptions
- **How do I solve problems I haven't seen before?** Apply systematic reasoning and debugging methodology
- **How do I weigh competing priorities?** Balance security, performance, cost, timelines, and organizational needs
- **How do I communicate decisions to stakeholders?** Translate technical trade-offs into business impact

Most real decisions don't have a clearly correct answer. What separates experienced practitioners is the ability to challenge their own assumptions and communicate trade-offs in terms stakeholders can act on. Kahneman's research on dual-process cognition [4] shows that expert judgment depends on both fast intuitive recognition and slower deliberate analysis.

## Decision Lens

Rather than following the stages sequentially, apply all four questions together to frame any problem or technical decision.

| Question | What it surfaces |
|----------|-----------------|
| **What is it?** | Context, scope, how it fits in the landscape |
| **How does it work?** | Mechanics, dependencies, constraints |
| **When and why?** | Trade-offs, pitfalls, appropriate use cases |
| **What if?** | Assumptions, risks, blind spots, stakeholder impact |

When facing a new tool, architecture decision, or unfamiliar problem, running through all four questions prevents narrow thinking. Orientation without judgment leads to superficial choices. Judgment without foundations leads to overconfident ones.

---

## Tying It Together

Both perspectives reinforce each other. Learning development builds the depth that makes the decision lens effective. The decision lens exercises the thinking that accelerates learning development.

This progression mirrors how expertise develops regardless of discipline [1][3]. Skipping stages creates gaps: patterns without foundations leads to misapplied solutions, foundations without constraints produces knowledge that doesn't transfer. Breadth of experience across domains strengthens the transfer of learning to new contexts [5].

- **Novices** need orientation: what exists, what it's called, where to look
- **Beginners** need foundations: how things work, what the rules are
- **Practitioners** need context: when to use what, how constraints shape decisions
- **Experts** operate with judgment: pattern recognition, contextual reasoning, navigating ambiguity

This repo complements but does not replace official documentation, formal training, hands-on experience, or your organization's own standards. It provides structured paths, consolidated reference, and practical framing that pure documentation lacks.

> The goal is to optimize the learning pathway that experience builds on, and to serve as a quick reference when navigating the intersection of technology, constraints, and stakeholder needs.

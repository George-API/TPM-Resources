## 1. Stakeholder Foundations

For full stakeholder identification, analysis frameworks (power/interest grid, influence/impact matrix), and engagement level definitions, see [Project Management](project_management.md) Section 6.

### Stakeholder Categories (Enterprise Context)

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Executive leadership** | C-suite, board members, senior executives, department heads |
| **Governance & oversight** | Audit committees, compliance officers, privacy commissioners, ombudsman offices |
| **End users / clients** | Service recipients, customers, community members, advocacy organizations |
| **Internal teams** | Project team, IT staff, operations, support, business units |
| **Vendors / contractors** | Third-party service providers, system integrators, managed service providers |
| **Labour representatives** | Employee representatives, labour organizations, staff associations |
| **Partner organizations** | Peer departments, partner agencies, affiliated organizations |

### Stakeholder Register Essentials

| Element | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Name / organization** | Who they are and where they sit |
| **Role & decision authority** | What they can approve, block, or influence |
| **Interests & motivations** | What they care about; what success looks like to them |
| **Influence & impact** | Power to affect the initiative; degree to which the initiative affects them |
| **Current engagement level** | Unaware, resistant, neutral, supportive, or leading |
| **Preferred communication** | Channel, frequency, format, level of detail |

### Key Principle

Stakeholder mapping is necessary but not sufficient. Knowing *who* matters is the foundation. The rest of this guide addresses *how* to work with them effectively.

## 2. Consulting & Advisory Mindset

### Internal Consulting vs External

| Dimension | External Consultant | Internal Consultant (PM/BA/TPM) |
|-----------|--------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Authority** | Contractual scope; limited organizational power | Organizational role; ongoing relationships, limited positional authority |
| **Credibility** | Imported expertise; "fresh eyes" premium | Earned over time; institutional knowledge is an asset |
| **Relationship** | Temporary; engagement-scoped | Permanent; reputation compounds |
| **Accountability** | Deliverable-based; exits after engagement | Lives with the consequences of recommendations |
| **Political exposure** | Can say things insiders can't | Must navigate internal politics carefully |

### The Trusted Advisor Spectrum

```
  Order Taker ──▶ Expert ──▶ Problem Solver ──▶ Trusted Advisor
       │              │            │                    │
  "Tell me what    "I know      "Let me help        "Let me help
   you want built"  the answer"   you think           you think about
                                  through this"       your business"
```

Most PMs and BAs operate at Expert or Problem Solver. Trusted Advisor status is earned, not claimed — it requires demonstrated judgment, reliability, and the willingness to challenge as well as support.

### The Trust Equation (Maister, Green, Galford)

```
                Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy
  Trust  =  ──────────────────────────────────────────
                         Self-Orientation
```

| Factor | Meaning | How to Build |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Credibility** | "I believe what they say" | Demonstrate expertise; be accurate; acknowledge limits |
| **Reliability** | "I can count on them" | Deliver on commitments; follow through; be consistent |
| **Intimacy** | "I feel safe with them" | Handle sensitive information carefully; show empathy; be discreet |
| **Self-orientation** (denominator) | "They focus on me, not themselves" | Listen before prescribing; put the stakeholder's interest ahead of your convenience |

High self-orientation destroys trust regardless of how competent, reliable, or empathetic you are.

### Advisory Principles

- **Diagnose before prescribing.** Understand the situation before offering solutions. Premature recommendations signal that you're more interested in your answer than their problem.
- **Earn the right to advise.** Start with smaller, lower-stakes contributions. Build credibility before challenging senior stakeholders on strategic issues.
- **Separate the person from the problem.** Disagree with the position, not the stakeholder. Frame challenges in terms of shared objectives.
- **Be useful, not right.** The goal is to help the stakeholder make a better decision, not to prove your analysis is superior.

## 3. Engagement Models

### Matching Approach to Context

| Mode | When to Use | Your Role | Risk If Mismatched |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Lead** | Stakeholder asks you to drive; problem is within your expertise; team lacks direction | Set direction, make recommendations, drive decisions | Overstepping if stakeholder wants to retain control |
| **Facilitate** | Multiple perspectives needed; conflict or ambiguity present; group needs to reach consensus | Design the process, remain neutral on content, guide the group | Being seen as biased if you inject your own opinion |
| **Advise** | Stakeholder owns the decision; needs expert input to inform their choice | Provide analysis, options, recommendations; stakeholder decides | Being blamed for the outcome if you overstate certainty |
| **Coach** | Stakeholder needs to develop their own capability; long-term growth over short-term efficiency | Ask questions, reflect back, support the stakeholder's reasoning | Frustration if stakeholder wants a direct answer |
| **Support** | Stakeholder is capable and aligned; needs logistics, coordination, or execution assistance | Remove blockers, coordinate, handle operational details | Being underutilized or disengaged if the work is beneath your capability |

### Engagement Selection Framework

```
                        Stakeholder capability
                      Low                High
                 ┌──────────────┬──────────────┐
  Problem   High │   Lead       │   Advise     │
  ambiguity      │              │              │
                 ├──────────────┼──────────────┤
            Low  │   Coach /    │   Support /  │
                 │   Facilitate │   Facilitate │
                 └──────────────┴──────────────┘
```

### Adapting Over Time

The right engagement mode changes as the initiative matures:
- **Initiation**: often Lead or Advise (stakeholders need direction and clarity)
- **Planning**: Facilitate (multiple inputs needed, consensus required)
- **Execution**: Support and Coach (teams execute, you remove blockers)
- **Closure**: Advise (lessons learned, transition recommendations)

## 4. Facilitation

### Workshop Design

| Element | Purpose | Considerations |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Clear objective** | What decision, output, or alignment must emerge | One primary objective; don't overload a single session |
| **Right participants** | People with knowledge, authority, or impact needed for the objective | Too few = missing perspectives; too many = diffusion of accountability |
| **Structured agenda** | Sequence of activities designed to reach the objective | Time-boxed segments; variety of formats (individual → pairs → group) |
| **Pre-work** | Preparation that makes session time productive | Distribute context materials 3-5 days in advance; be specific about what to review |
| **Facilitation tools** | Techniques to draw out input and manage discussion | Brainstorming, dot voting, affinity grouping, silent writing, round-robin |
| **Documented outputs** | Captured decisions, actions, open items | Assign a note-taker; distribute outputs within 48 hours |

### Facilitation Techniques

| Technique | When to Use | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Silent writing / brainwriting** | When dominant voices suppress quieter participants | Everyone writes ideas individually before group discussion |
| **Round-robin** | When equal input is needed from all participants | Each person speaks in turn; no interruptions |
| **Dot voting** | When the group needs to prioritize quickly | Each person gets N votes to place on options; reveals collective priority |
| **Parking lot** | When valid but off-topic issues arise | Capture for later; keep the session focused on its objective |
| **Affinity grouping** | When large volumes of ideas need organizing | Group related items into themes; label themes collaboratively |
| **Fishbowl** | When a subset needs to debate while others observe | Inner circle discusses; outer circle listens; rotate in |
| **RACI exercise** | When roles and responsibilities are unclear or contested | Collaboratively assign Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed per deliverable |
| **Pre-mortem** | When the group needs to identify risks proactively | "Assume this initiative has failed. What went wrong?" |

### Managing Group Dynamics

| Dynamic | Response |
|---------|----------|
| **Dominant participant** | Acknowledge contribution, redirect: "Thank you — let's hear from others." Use structured techniques (silent writing, round-robin) |
| **Silent participant** | Direct invitation: "We haven't heard your perspective on this yet." Use pair work before group discussion |
| **Side conversations** | Pause and redirect: "Let's keep one conversation going." Address if persistent |
| **Conflict** | Name it constructively: "We have two different views here. Let's understand both before deciding." Separate interests from positions |
| **Scope drift** | Parking lot: "That's important — let's capture it and address it in the right forum." Return to the agenda |
| **Decision avoidance** | Make the decision explicit: "We need to decide X by the end of this session. What information is missing?" |
| **Energy loss** | Break, change format, or move to a more interactive activity. Long lectures kill workshops |

### Virtual Facilitation Adjustments

- **Shorter sessions**: 90 minutes maximum; schedule breaks for longer workshops
- **Higher structure**: Virtual participants disengage faster; tighter time-boxing and more frequent interaction
- **Visual collaboration tools**: Shared whiteboards, sticky-note tools, collaborative documents replace physical materials
- **Cameras on**: Encourage video for engagement and social cues, but respect bandwidth and fatigue constraints
- **Explicit turn-taking**: Chat-based input, hand-raise features, and direct calls compensate for lost body-language cues

## 5. Negotiation

### Principled Negotiation (Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes*)

| Principle | Meaning | Application |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Separate people from the problem** | Address the issue without attacking the relationship | "I understand your team's concern about timeline. Let's look at what's driving it." |
| **Focus on interests, not positions** | Positions are what people say they want; interests are why they want it | "You're asking for dedicated resources [position]. What outcome are you trying to protect? [interest]" |
| **Generate options for mutual gain** | Expand the pie before dividing it | Brainstorm multiple solutions before evaluating; avoid binary either/or framing |
| **Use objective criteria** | Base the agreement on fair standards, not power | Industry benchmarks, organizational policies, precedent, technical constraints |

### Key Negotiation Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Use |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| **BATNA** (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement) | Your best option if negotiation fails | Know your BATNA before entering; it defines your walk-away point |
| **ZOPA** (Zone of Possible Agreement) | The range where both parties can agree | If there's no ZOPA, no deal is possible; focus on expanding interests to create one |
| **Reservation point** | The worst deal you'd accept | Don't reveal it; use BATNA to strengthen it |
| **Anchoring** | The first number or position sets a reference point | Make a well-reasoned first proposal; counter unreasonable anchors with data |
| **Logrolling** | Trading concessions on issues of different priority | "I'll give on X (lower priority for me) if you can give on Y (higher priority for me)" |

### Common Negotiation Scenarios in PM/BA

| Scenario | Typical Tension | Approach |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| **Scope vs timeline** | Stakeholders want everything by the deadline | Quantify trade-offs; present prioritized options; use MoSCoW |
| **Resource allocation** | Multiple projects compete for the same people | Escalate to portfolio level; use data on utilization and impact |
| **Vendor contract terms** | Vendor wants favorable terms; buyer wants protection | Benchmark against market; focus on risk-sharing, not winner/loser |
| **Priority conflicts** | Two business units each claim their initiative is top priority | Facilitate a structured prioritization using agreed criteria; escalate to shared governance |
| **Technical approach** | Engineering and business disagree on approach | Frame in terms of business risk and value; let data arbitrate |
| **Budget reduction** | Budget cut requires scope reduction | Present options with explicit consequences: "With X budget, we can deliver A and B but not C" |

### Negotiation Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem |
|-------------|---------|
| Positional bargaining | Entrenching in positions creates win/lose dynamics; erodes relationships |
| Avoiding negotiation | Accepting unfavorable terms to avoid conflict; builds resentment and unsustainable commitments |
| Splitting the difference | Appears fair but rewards extreme opening positions and penalizes reasonable ones |
| Negotiating against yourself | Making concessions before the other party responds; weakens your position |
| Treating every interaction as a negotiation | Over-applying negotiation tactics damages collaborative relationships |

## 6. Influence Without Authority

### Why It Matters

PMs, BAs, and TPMs rarely have direct authority over the people they need to mobilize. Influence is the primary mechanism for getting things done in matrix organizations, cross-functional teams, and stakeholder relationships.

### Influence Levers

| Lever | Mechanism | How to Build |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Expertise** | People follow those who know what they're talking about | Demonstrate competence through quality work; share knowledge generously; be accurate |
| **Relationships** | People cooperate with those they trust and like | Invest in relationships before you need something; be genuinely interested in others' work |
| **Reciprocity** | People return favors | Help others succeed; share credit; volunteer for unglamorous but important work |
| **Credibility** | People act on recommendations from those with a track record | Deliver on commitments; be honest about what you don't know; admit mistakes |
| **Framing** | How you present information shapes how people perceive it | Lead with what matters to them; connect your request to their goals and priorities |
| **Coalition** | Collective influence exceeds individual influence | Align key stakeholders before the meeting; build informal agreement before formal decisions |
| **Organizational currency** | What you can offer that others value | Information, access, visibility, resources, support, recognition |

### Coalition Building

```
  Identify ──▶ Align ──▶ Coordinate ──▶ Mobilize
     │            │            │              │
  Who needs    1:1 pre-     Agree on      Present a
  to support   meetings     messaging     unified
  this?        to align     and approach  position
```

- **Map the decision.** Who decides? Who influences the decider? Who can block?
- **Start with allies.** Build momentum with supporters before approaching skeptics.
- **Pre-wire decisions.** Critical decisions should never be surprises in the room. Align key players beforehand.
- **Address objections privately.** Give skeptics a chance to raise concerns 1:1 before the group forum.

### Influence Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem |
|-------------|---------|
| Relying on escalation as a first resort | Erodes relationships; signals inability to resolve issues at your level |
| Manipulation vs influence | Withholding information or engineering outcomes without transparency destroys trust when discovered |
| Influence without substance | Relationship skills without expertise or delivery track record are unsustainable |
| Ignoring informal power | Focusing only on the org chart misses the people who actually shape decisions |

## 7. Managing Competing Interests

### Why Interests Conflict

Stakeholders have legitimately different priorities. A CFO optimizes for cost. A CISO optimizes for security. A product owner optimizes for speed. A compliance officer optimizes for risk reduction. These are not pathologies — they are structural tensions inherent in any organization.

### Conflict Types

| Type | Characteristics | Response |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Priority conflict** | Multiple stakeholders each believe their need is most urgent | Structured prioritization using agreed criteria; escalate to governance if no resolution |
| **Scope conflict** | Stakeholders want mutually exclusive features or approaches | Trade-off analysis; present options with consequences; facilitate the decision |
| **Resource conflict** | Competing demand for the same people, budget, or infrastructure | Portfolio-level visibility; data-driven allocation; escalate if beyond your authority |
| **Value conflict** | Fundamental disagreement about what matters (speed vs quality, innovation vs stability) | Surface the underlying values explicitly; find the organizational principle that arbitrates; escalate if irreconcilable |
| **Information conflict** | Stakeholders have different facts or interpret the same facts differently | Establish a shared fact base before debating conclusions |

### Trade-Off Facilitation Process

```
  Surface ──▶ Clarify ──▶ Evaluate ──▶ Decide ──▶ Communicate
     │            │            │           │            │
  Name the     Understand   Assess       Select      Explain
  tension      the interests impact of    option &    rationale
  explicitly   behind each  each option  document    to all
               position                  trade-offs  parties
```

### Structured Disagreement Principles

- **Legitimize the tension.** "Both perspectives are valid. The question is which trade-off best serves the organization right now."
- **Separate diagnosis from solution.** Get agreement on the problem before debating the answer.
- **Use criteria, not authority.** "Based on our strategic priorities and the data, Option A scores higher on..." is more durable than "The VP wants Option A."
- **Document what was decided and what was traded away.** Stakeholders accept trade-offs more readily when they know their concern was heard and explicitly weighed.
- **Revisit if assumptions change.** A decision made under one set of constraints should be reopened if those constraints materially change.

### Escalation Strategy

| Level | When to Escalate | How |
|-------|------------------|-----|
| **Peer resolution** | Default; most conflicts should resolve here | Facilitated discussion, shared criteria, data |
| **Manager escalation** | Peers cannot agree; organizational authority is needed | Present the disagreement fairly (both sides), the options, your recommendation |
| **Steering committee** | Cross-program conflict; strategic trade-off; resource allocation beyond any single manager's authority | Formal decision paper with options, impact analysis, and recommendation |
| **Executive sponsor** | Project-threatening impasse; political dynamics; decisions with significant reputational or financial risk | 1:1 briefing with clear framing of the decision and its consequences |

## 8. Executive Communication & Managing Up

### Executive Communication Principles

- **Lead with the decision or ask.** Executives have limited time. State what you need from them in the first 30 seconds.
- **Summarize, don't narrate.** Provide the conclusion first; have supporting detail ready if asked.
- **Quantify where possible.** "3 weeks behind schedule with $50K budget risk" is actionable; "there are some challenges" is not.
- **Bring options, not just problems.** "We have a problem and here are three options" is more useful than "we have a problem."
- **Know your audience's priorities.** Frame recommendations in terms of what the executive cares about (cost, timeline, risk, reputation, strategic alignment).

### Executive Briefing Format

| Component | Content | Time |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Situation** | Where things stand (1-2 sentences) | 30 seconds |
| **Key issue** | The decision, risk, or blocker that needs attention | 30 seconds |
| **Options** | 2-3 alternatives with trade-offs | 1-2 minutes |
| **Recommendation** | What you recommend and why | 30 seconds |
| **Ask** | What you need from them (decision, resources, air cover) | 15 seconds |
| **Supporting detail** | Data, analysis, context — only if asked | As needed |

### Managing Sponsors

| Responsibility | Practice |
|---------------|----------|
| **Keep them informed** | No surprises. Bad news delivered early is a problem; bad news delivered late is a crisis |
| **Make them look good** | Prepare them for steering committees, board presentations, and peer conversations with clear talking points |
| **Use their authority wisely** | Escalate only what needs their level; resolve what you can at your level |
| **Coach them on their role** | Sponsors don't always know what good sponsorship looks like. Help them understand what you need from them |
| **Protect their time** | Be concise, prepared, and decisive in your interactions. Respect that their attention is a scarce resource |

### Status Reporting for Executives

| Practice | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| **Exception-based reporting** | Report what's off-track or needs attention; don't walk through everything that's fine |
| **Consistent format** | Same structure every time; executives scan, not read. Predictability enables speed |
| **Forward-looking** | What's happening next is more useful than what happened last week |
| **Honest on risk** | Understating risk to avoid difficult conversations erodes trust faster than delivering bad news |

## 9. Difficult Conversations

### When Difficult Conversations Are Necessary

- Delivering bad news (delays, budget overruns, failed initiatives)
- Saying no to a powerful stakeholder
- Challenging a flawed assumption or decision
- Surfacing risks that leadership doesn't want to hear
- Addressing non-performance on the team or with a vendor
- Raising ethical or compliance concerns

### Preparation Framework

| Step | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1 | **Clarify your purpose.** What outcome do you need from this conversation? What's the minimum acceptable result? |
| 2 | **Separate facts from interpretation.** "The deployment was delayed by 3 weeks" (fact) vs "The team didn't try hard enough" (interpretation) |
| 3 | **Anticipate their perspective.** How will they see this? What are their interests, concerns, and likely objections? |
| 4 | **Plan your opening.** Lead with facts and impact, not blame. State the issue directly but respectfully |
| 5 | **Prepare for emotion.** Theirs and yours. Plan how to respond if the conversation becomes adversarial or emotional |
| 6 | **Know your ask.** What specific action, decision, or acknowledgment do you need by the end? |

### Delivering Bad News

| Principle | Practice |
|-----------|---------|
| **Early** | Deliver bad news as soon as you know it. Delay makes it worse |
| **Direct** | State the issue clearly. Don't bury it in qualifiers, context, or good news |
| **Owned** | Take responsibility where appropriate. "We underestimated the complexity" not "the requirements kept changing" |
| **Actionable** | Present the problem with a proposed path forward. "We're three weeks behind. Here's our recovery plan" |
| **Private first** | Brief the most affected stakeholder privately before a group setting where possible |

### Saying No Constructively

```
  Acknowledge ──▶ Explain ──▶ Offer Alternative
       │              │              │
  "I understand     "Given our     "What I can do
   why this is      current        is..."
   important        constraints,
   to you"          this isn't
                    feasible
                    because..."
```

- Frame around constraints, not refusal: "Given our current scope and timeline, adding X would delay Y by three weeks."
- Offer what you *can* do, not just what you can't.
- If the stakeholder has the authority to override, present the trade-off clearly and let them decide.

### Challenging Assumptions

| Approach | Language |
|----------|---------|
| **Question, don't assert** | "What's our evidence for that assumption?" rather than "That assumption is wrong" |
| **Use data** | "The data from the last three projects shows..." rather than "I don't think that's realistic" |
| **Hypothetical framing** | "What would happen if that assumption turned out to be wrong?" invites exploration without confrontation |
| **Reference external examples** | "In similar initiatives, the typical timeline was X" provides cover for the challenge |
| **Name the risk** | "If we proceed on that assumption and it doesn't hold, the impact would be..." makes the stakes concrete |

## 10. Trust & Credibility

### Building Trust

| Stage | Focus | Actions |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Initial contact** | Competence signal | Be prepared, be punctual, demonstrate you've done your homework |
| **Early engagement** | Reliability | Deliver on small commitments; follow up on action items; be responsive |
| **Working relationship** | Depth | Share relevant information proactively; acknowledge mistakes; handle sensitive information carefully |
| **Established trust** | Candor | Offer honest assessments, including uncomfortable ones; challenge constructively; advocate for their interests |

### Trust Accelerators and Destroyers

| Accelerators | Destroyers |
|-------------|-----------|
| Delivering on commitments consistently | Over-promising and under-delivering |
| Proactive communication (especially bad news) | Surprises, especially ones that could have been flagged earlier |
| Acknowledging what you don't know | Faking expertise or certainty |
| Handling confidential information with care | Sharing sensitive information inappropriately |
| Giving credit to others | Taking credit for others' work |
| Being consistent across audiences | Saying different things to different people |
| Following through on action items | Dropping commitments without acknowledgment |

### Trust Recovery

Trust is asymmetric: slow to build, fast to lose, and harder to rebuild than to build the first time.

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | **Acknowledge the breach.** Don't minimize, deflect, or explain it away |
| 2 | **Take responsibility.** Own your part, even if external factors contributed |
| 3 | **Explain what happened.** Context (not excuses) helps the other party understand |
| 4 | **State what you'll do differently.** Specific, observable commitments |
| 5 | **Demonstrate through action.** Rebuilding trust requires sustained, consistent follow-through over time |

### Credibility Across Audiences

| Audience | Credibility Signals |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Technical teams** | Understanding of technical constraints, willingness to learn, not oversimplifying, respecting engineering judgment |
| **Business stakeholders** | Speaking their language (value, cost, risk, outcomes), understanding their operational reality |
| **Executives** | Concise communication, strategic thinking, ability to synthesize complex information, sound judgment |
| **Vendors** | Professionalism, clear expectations, fair treatment, knowledge of contract terms and market |
| **Regulated environment stakeholders** | Understanding of institutional constraints, policy compliance, accountability frameworks |

## 11. Regulated & Institutional Engagement

For detailed regulated-environment change management practices, see [Change Management](change_management.md) Section 12.

### Institutional Engagement Principles

| Principle | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Transparency by default** | Communications and documents may be subject to freedom of information requests; draft accordingly |
| **Accountability structures** | External scrutiny (auditors, oversight bodies, public reporting) shapes how engagement must be documented |
| **Formal consultation obligations** | Mandated engagement periods, advisory committees, and structured feedback processes add timeline and procedural requirements |
| **Service obligation** | Initiatives serve organizational mandate and stakeholder interests, not just internal efficiency |

### Institutional Stakeholder Dynamics

| Dynamic | Management Approach |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Leadership cycles** | Executive transitions and board turnover can shift priorities mid-initiative; build resilience into sponsorship through coalition, not individual dependence |
| **Budget cycles** | Annual budget and multi-year capital planning create fixed windows for investment decisions; align initiative timing to these cycles |
| **Political and reputational risk** | Institutional decisions carry external visibility; assess reputational impact alongside technical and financial impact |
| **Labour relations** | Collective agreements may govern how process, tool, and role changes are introduced; engage labour representatives early as partners |
| **Multilingual requirements** | Communications, training, and interfaces may need to be available in multiple languages where applicable |
| **Accessibility** | All engagement channels and materials must meet applicable accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1 AA minimum) |

### Formal Engagement Channels

| Channel | Use |
|---------|-----|
| **Public meetings / town halls** | Required engagement for initiatives with broad stakeholder impact |
| **Advisory committees** | Standing bodies mandated by legislation or policy; regular input on specific domains (accessibility, privacy, community) |
| **Formal comment periods** | Structured windows for written stakeholder submissions |
| **Steering committees** | Executive oversight with documented terms of reference, decision authority, and reporting cadence |
| **Transparency portals** | Public-facing project dashboards and status updates (may be required under open data or disclosure policies) |

## 12. Consulting Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Countermeasure |
|-------------|---------|----------------|
| **Solutioning before listening** | Prescribing an answer before understanding the problem; signals self-orientation | Discipline yourself to diagnose first; restate the problem back before offering solutions |
| **Being the bottleneck** | Inserting yourself into every decision; slows the initiative and creates dependency | Delegate decisions to the lowest appropriate level; empower teams |
| **Over-promising** | Committing to outcomes you can't guarantee to please stakeholders in the moment | Promise process, not outcomes: "I'll assess and come back with options by Friday" |
| **Avoiding conflict** | Letting disagreements fester to maintain surface harmony | Address tensions early and directly; unresolved conflict doesn't disappear, it compounds |
| **Telling stakeholders what they want to hear** | Filtering bad news or inflating confidence to avoid discomfort | Honest assessments build trust; optimistic ones build risk |
| **Confusing activity with value** | Producing documents, running meetings, and tracking actions without advancing the decision or outcome | Regularly ask: "Is this activity moving us toward a decision or delivery?" |
| **Expertise hoarding** | Keeping knowledge to yourself to maintain relevance | Share knowledge freely; your value is judgment and facilitation, not information monopoly |
| **Escalating too early** | Using management authority before attempting peer resolution | Exhaust collaborative options first; escalation should be the last resort, not the first |
| **Ignoring the informal organization** | Working only through official channels and org-chart relationships | Map and engage informal influencers, connectors, and opinion leaders |
| **One-size-fits-all engagement** | Using the same communication style and engagement approach for every stakeholder | Adapt your style, level of detail, and channel to each audience |

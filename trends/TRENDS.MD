**Report Date**: 2026

## Executive Summary

This report synthesizes insights from leading industry research covering critical IT and technology strategy trends shaping 2026. Based on analysis of eight authoritative sources, the findings reveal a technology landscape defined by AI-driven transformation, escalating cybersecurity risks, and the imperative for integrated governance frameworks.

**Key Findings:**
- **94%** of global executives identify AI as the most significant cybersecurity driver in 2026
- **65%** of large organizations cite supply chain vulnerabilities as their greatest cyber resilience barrier (up from 54% in 2025)
- **73%** of respondents were directly affected by cyber-enabled fraud in 2025
- **80%** of analytics professionals now use AI daily in workflows (up from ~30% in 2024)
- Enterprises are deferring **25%** of planned AI spending into 2027 awaiting measurable ROI

**Strategic Imperatives:**
1. Integrate AI governance with measurable ROI frameworks
2. Embed security-by-design across development lifecycles
3. Build resilient data foundations for trusted AI
4. Strengthen integrated risk portfolio management
5. Align engineering delivery with strategic transformation goals

## 1. Cybersecurity & Digital Risk

### 1.1 AI-Driven Threat Landscape and Fraud Proliferation

Cyber-enabled fraud has risen sharply, in some cases overtaking ransomware as the most pervasive global cyber threat. Security leaders identify AI as the most consequential influence on cyber risk in 2026, with immediate vulnerabilities tied to generative and automated attacks.

#### Core Metrics
*Source: WEF Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2026 (Survey of 804 leaders across 92 countries)*

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **AI as most significant cybersecurity driver** | **94%** | Executives expecting AI to be the most significant driver of change in 2026 |
| **AI-related vulnerabilities growth** | **87%** | Cybersecurity leaders reporting AI vulnerabilities grew faster than any other risk category in 2025 |
| **Cyber-enabled fraud impact** | **73%** | Respondents directly affected or knowing someone affected by cyber-enabled fraud in 2025 |
| **AI tool security assessment** | **64%** | Organizations now assessing AI tool security before deployment (up from 37% in 2025) |
| **Generative AI data leak concerns** | **34%** | Organizations reporting concerns over data leaks from generative AI |
| **Adversarial AI capability concerns** | **29%** | Organizations citing advancing adversarial AI capabilities as top concerns |
| **AI tools adoption for cybersecurity** | **77%** | Organizations that have adopted AI tools for cybersecurity |
| **AI for phishing detection** | **52%** | Organizations using AI tools for phishing detection |
| **AI for intrusion detection** | **46%** | Organizations using AI for intrusion detection and anomaly response |

### 1.2 Systemic Cyber Risk and Supply Chain Exposure

Interconnected risks — cyber, fraud, supply chain, geopolitical friction — now define enterprise vulnerability. Third-party ecosystem visibility and simulation exercises remain underutilized despite increasing systemic risk.

#### Core Metrics
*Source: WEF Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2026*

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **Supply chain as greatest barrier** | **65%** | Large organizations citing third-party and supply chain vulnerabilities as greatest barrier to cyber resilience (up from 54% in 2025) |
| **Geopolitical risk integration** | **64%** | Organizations explicitly accounting for geopolitically motivated cyberattacks in risk models |
| **Low confidence in national response** | **31%** | Respondents expressing low confidence in country's ability to respond to major cyber incidents targeting critical infrastructure |
| **Regional confidence disparity** | **84% vs 13%** | High confidence in Middle East & North Africa (84%) vs. low confidence in Latin America & Caribbean (13%) |

Cybersecurity requires enterprise-wide governance and shared responsibility beyond the network perimeter.

## 2. Application & Software Security

### 2.1 OWASP Top 10: 2025 Application Security Risks

The 2025 OWASP Top 10 signals persistent and evolving application security risks, with supply chain and design-centric risks reflecting systemic development challenges. Traditional code-centric flaw lists are giving way to lifecycle and dependency concerns.

#### Top 10 Application Security Risks (2025)

1. **Broken Access Control** (A01:2025)
2. **Security Misconfiguration** (A02:2025)
3. **Software Supply Chain Failures** (A03:2025)
4. **Cryptographic Failures** (A04:2025)
5. **Injection** (A05:2025)
6. **Insecure Design** (A06:2025)
7. **Authentication Failures** (A07:2025)
8. **Software/Data Integrity Failures** (A08:2025)
9. **Inadequate Logging/Alerting** (A09:2025)
10. **Mishandling Exceptional Conditions** (A10:2025)

#### Core Metrics
*Source: OWASP Top 10: 2025*

| Risk Category | Rank Change | CWEs | % Affected | Key Details |
|--------------|-------------|------|------------|-------------|
| **Broken Access Control** | Remains #1 | 40 | ~3.73% | Includes broader coverage (SSRF, token manipulation) |
| **Security Misconfiguration** | #5 → #2 | 16 | ~3.00% | Driven by cloud misconfigurations, open storage buckets, default credentials |
| **Software Supply Chain Failures** | New category | 5 | Low occurrence | Highest average exploit & impact scores despite lower frequency |
| **Cryptographic Failures** | #2 → #4 | 32 | ~3.80% | Improved awareness but remains persistent risk |
| **Injection** | #3 → #5 | 38 | Present in 100%* | Remains serious; present in 100% of tested apps (for some forms) |
| **Authentication Failures** | #6 → #7 | 36 | — | Authentication and session management issues |
| **Mishandling Exceptional Conditions** | New category | 24 | — | Poor error handling, failing open, information leaks from error messages |

**Note**: The 2025 OWASP Top 10 is based on approximately **589 Common Weakness Enumerations (CWEs)**, up from roughly **400 in 2021**.

#### Key Trends

- **Supply chain and design-centric risks** reflect systemic development challenges
- **Traditional code-centric flaw lists** are giving way to lifecycle and dependency concerns
- **Security Misconfiguration** rose dramatically from #5 to #2, driven by cloud adoption
- **Software Supply Chain Failures** represents a new category with highest impact scores

## 4. Technology Investment & Market Forecasts

### 4.1 Forrester 2026 Technology & Security Predictions

#### AI Hype to Measurable Outcomes

Forrester predicts the AI hype period is ending, with enterprises deferring approximately **25%** of planned AI spend into 2027 awaiting measurable ROI. Fewer than one-third of decision makers can currently tie AI to financial growth, tightening CFO-led governance.

#### Core Metrics
*Source: Forrester 2026 Technology & Security Predictions*

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **AI spending deferral** | **25%** | Enterprises deferring planned AI spending into 2027 due to financial discipline and ROI demands |
| **Quantum security budget allocation** | **>5%** | Expected to exceed 5% of overall IT security budgets in 2026 |
| **Asia-Pacific post-quantum investment** | **>90%** | Firms expected to invest in post-quantum technologies in 2026 |
| **Software share of cybersecurity budgets** | **40.2%** | Software now accounts for 40.2% of cybersecurity budgets, surpassing personnel costs by ~11 percentage points |
| **Cybersecurity budget increases** | **55%** | Global security and tech leaders forecasting significant budget increases; within that group: 15% expect >10% increases, 40% expect 5–10% increases |
| **Global technology spending (2025)** | **US$4.9T** | Projected to reach US$4.9 trillion in 2025, growing 5.6% over 2024 (US$4.7 trillion) |
| **Software + IT services share** | **66%** | Will account for 66% of global tech spend in 2025 |
| **Neocloud revenue projection** | **US$20B** | Neoclouds (AI workload specialization providers) projected to generate $20 billion in revenue |

#### Emerging Security Priorities

- **Quantum security allocation** (>5% of security budget) and cryptographic discovery tooling are growth areas in 2026
- **Specialized infrastructure** (e.g., neoclouds for GPU orchestration and sovereign AI) expands beyond hyperscaler dominance

Forrester's guidance emphasizes tight financial scrutiny, governance integration, and measurable value delivery for emerging tech investments.

## 9. Strategic Recommendations

### A. Integrate AI Governance and ROI Measurement

Prioritise structured AI governance frameworks with CFO-level oversight to ensure measurable business benefit.

### B. Embed Security Early in Development

Adopt secure-by-design principles, supply chain risk verification, and digital provenance from planning through deployment.

### C. Build Resilient Data Foundations

Invest in data quality, lineage, and observability to underpin trusted AI and analytics.

### D. Strengthen Risk Portfolio Management

Coordinate risk functions across cyber, operational, and third-party domains to reduce systemic exposure.

### E. Align Engineering with Strategic Value

Use strategic trend frameworks (Gartner, Deloitte) to align engineering delivery with enterprise-wide transformation goals.

## 10. Conclusion

Across the 2026 landscape, AI, security, data trust, and resilience are the cornerstones of IT strategy. The transition from pilot projects to measurable, strategic impact defines success. Leaders must balance investment discipline with innovation, embed governance deeply, and unify risk practices across the enterprise to maintain competitive advantage.

**Key Takeaways:**
- AI transformation requires governance frameworks with measurable ROI
- Supply chain and third-party risks demand integrated risk management
- Security must be embedded across development lifecycles, not bolted on
- Data quality and observability remain foundational despite AI acceleration
- Strategic alignment between engineering delivery and enterprise transformation is critical
